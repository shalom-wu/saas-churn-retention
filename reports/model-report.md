# Churn Model Report

Performance, interpretation, and — importantly — limitations of the churn
prediction models. Numbers below are from the held-out test set (20% of
customers, stratified split, `random_state=42`); the full metrics are in
[`model-metrics.json`](model-metrics.json) and are regenerated by
`python -m src.run_pipeline`.

## Setup

- **Target:** churn label (26.5% positive — imbalanced enough that accuracy
  is a misleading metric, not so extreme that resampling is needed).
- **Features:** tenure, monthly charges, add-on service count, and one-hot
  encodings of contract, payment, demographic and service columns.
  `TotalCharges` is excluded: it is almost exactly `tenure x MonthlyCharges`,
  so it adds collinearity without adding information.
- **Imbalance handling:** class weighting (logistic) and
  `scale_pos_weight` (XGBoost) rather than resampling — both models are used
  as *rankers* (who is riskiest?) rather than hard classifiers, so
  well-ordered probabilities matter more than the 0.5 threshold.
- **Models:** L2 logistic regression baseline, then XGBoost
  (300 trees, depth 3, learning rate 0.05).

## Results

| Metric (test set) | Logistic regression | XGBoost |
|---|---|---|
| ROC-AUC | 0.838 | **0.844** |
| PR-AUC | 0.627 | **0.662** |
| Precision @ 0.5 | 0.508 | 0.518 |
| Recall @ 0.5 | 0.778 | 0.789 |
| F1 @ 0.5 | 0.615 | 0.626 |
| Accuracy | 0.741 | 0.749 |

Logistic 5-fold CV ROC-AUC on the training set: **0.845 ± 0.012** — the test
score sits inside the CV band, so the split isn't flattering us.

### Operating points that matter: campaign depth

A retention team never contacts "everyone above probability 0.5"; it has
budget for the riskiest N%. Ranking customers by XGBoost score:

| Campaign depth | Contacted | Precision | Recall | Lift vs random |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Top 10% | 141 | 76.6% | 28.9% | 2.9x |
| Top 20% | 282 | 67.4% | 50.8% | 2.5x |
| Top 30% | 423 | 58.9% | 66.6% | 2.2x |

Reading: if the team can only reach 20% of the book, 2 in 3 of the customers
they contact are genuine churn risks, and they reach half of all churners —
2.5x better than untargeted outreach. These depths are what the ROI model in
the strategy deck is built on.

## What drives the predictions

Both the SHAP analysis (XGBoost) and the standardized coefficients
(logistic) agree with the descriptive EDA, which is reassuring — the model
isn't finding some exotic pattern the business can't act on:

1. **Contract type** — month-to-month is the single strongest churn signal;
   two-year contracts are strongly protective.
2. **Tenure** — risk is heavily front-loaded in the first year.
3. **Fiber-optic internet** (the premium product) pushes churn *up*, which
   with the price effect below suggests a price/value problem in the
   premium tier rather than a product-mix curiosity.
4. **No online security / no tech support** — customers without support-type
   add-ons churn more.
5. **Electronic check payment** — a manual payment step correlates with
   churn even after controlling for contract.

One coefficient needs care: `MonthlyCharges` shows a *negative* logistic
coefficient while fiber-optic shows a large positive one. The two are highly
correlated (fiber is expensive), so the linear model splits the "expensive
fiber" effect across the two features; neither coefficient should be read in
isolation. SHAP on the tree model shows the same interaction more cleanly:
high monthly charges push predictions toward churn overall.

## Why so little tuning?

A bigger XGBoost (500 trees, depth 4) scored **worse** on held-out data
(ROC-AUC 0.833 vs 0.844) than the shallow configuration, and early stopping
converged at ~50 trees. With ~5,600 training rows of mostly-categorical
data, this problem rewards restraint; an exhaustive grid search would mostly
tune noise. The honest headline is that **gradient boosting buys only ~0.006
ROC-AUC over a well-specified logistic regression** here — the signal lives
in a handful of features, not in deep interactions.

## Limitations

1. **Snapshot data, not event history.** The dataset is one cross-section:
   we never observe *when* a customer churned relative to their features. A
   production system would train on time-stamped events with
   point-in-time features and be validated out-of-time, not just
   out-of-sample.
2. **Label window is unspecified.** The churn flag says "left recently"
   without defining the window. Treating the 26.5% as a cohort share (as the
   LTV model does) is defensible; treating it as a monthly rate would be
   absurd (it would imply the book turns over four times a year).
3. **No behavioral features.** Usage frequency, support tickets, NPS,
   payment failures — the strongest churn predictors in real subscription
   businesses — aren't in this dataset. The ~0.84 AUC ceiling here is a
   feature-set ceiling, not a modeling ceiling.
4. **Telecom data in SaaS framing.** Structure transfers (contracts,
   add-ons, recurring billing), but the specific coefficients would not
   carry over to any particular SaaS business. The *method* is the portable
   part.
5. **Correlation, not causation.** The model says who is likely to churn,
   not what will happen if we intervene. Contract type in particular is
   partly self-selection — commitment-averse customers choose month-to-month.
   The intervention ROI in the deck therefore uses deliberately conservative
   uplift assumptions, and any rollout should be A/B tested.
